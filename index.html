Yale University Staffing: Fall 2002-Fall 2021

Ellen Connell, INFO 696, Spring 2023

Introduction

In recent years, “administrative bloat” has been a frequent focus of op-eds, blog posts and critiques including an article by the Yale News in November 2021 that cited faculty members lamenting the “proliferation of administrators” in “two decades of rapid expansion” as effectively “outpacing growth in student enrollment.”

To explore this articles’ thesis pulled from the total headcounts cited in Yale’s financial reports, I compiled and charted Integrated Postsecondary Education Data System (IPEDS) surveys, pulling student enrollment data from the Fall Enrollment surveys and employee data from the Fall Staff surveys for the period identified in the Yale News article: Fall 2002 - Fall 2021.

A period of growth, overall

Except for temporary dips in student enrollment due to the 2007-2008 financial crisis and COVID-19 pandemic, student enrollment has steadily climbed at Yale from Fall 2002 - Fall 20221, with total staffing following student enrollments’ upward climb (Figure 1).

Filtering total staff by type, defined by a position’s primary function as either instructional/research or not, illustrates that both the academic and non academic staff have both increased over the period.

Segmenting by staff type also illustrates that non academic staff steadily exceeds the number of instructional or research staff over the period with non-instructional staff increasing from 4,279 in Fall 2002 to 6,261 in Fall 2021 compared to 1309 and 2153.

But how does this increase in staff compare to the increase in student enrollment over the period?

An ‘outsized’ increase in administrators?

To explore the relationship between student and staff FTEs over the period, I calculated and plotted the student to staff ratio, segmented by staff type, with the assumption that if the growth in staff is directly proportional to growth in enrollment, the ratio of students to staff should remain the consistent over the period.

For all staff, the ratio generally holds between 2 and 1.5 students per 1 staff member, with the greatest change in the years where enrollment is drastically reduced in 2008 and 2020, respectively.

Filtering by staff type and charting the changes in ratio and between instructional and non-instructional staff illustrates that ratio for both instructional and non instructional staff reduces over the period, signaling that the growth in both types of staff has outpaced the growth in students (Figure 2).

Instructional staff grown the most

However, it is actually the student to instructional staff ratio that shows the greatest variability and net change over the period with a dropping of 8.6 in Fall 2001 to 6.7 in Fall 2021 compared to 2.63 and 2.31, respectively.

Charting the cumulative percentage change further illustrates that contrary to the reports of the ‘proliferation of administrators’ in the ‘last two decades’ at Yale, the growth in instructional and research dedicated staff has grown the most in the last 20 years (Figure 3).

How does Yale compare?

Although IPEDS data did not support the direct claim that the last two decades has seen a significant change in the number of administrators, Yale’s student to staff ratio is significantly smaller than both their average peer institution (research intensive institutions) and the overall average of all degree-granting, four-year nonprofit institutions (Figure 4), signaling that there is in fact a “proliferation” of administrators at Yale in comparison to others.

Furthermore, the difference in the student to staff ratio between the average of all research institutions vs. all institutions illustrates that the number of staff to students varies by institutional type with research institutions providing expanded programs, research infrastructure, and clinical staff.

Why it matters

To members of the Yale faculty, a bloated administration size inflicts unnecessary costs and interferes with faculty teaching, spreading the burden of leadership and adding excessive regulation.

As Professor David Bromwich notes, “Yale, like many other universities, clearly now wants to be known not only as a place for teaching, learning, and research, but also as a home, a community, an innovative corporate entity. The swollen self-image requires expanded oversight, and administrators are the overseers.”
